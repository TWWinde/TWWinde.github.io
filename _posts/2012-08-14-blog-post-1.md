This post derives **Flow Matching** and **Diffusion Models** from first principles. We start from the fundamental question of generative modeling, build the necessary mathematical tools, and arrive at practical training objectives.

## 1. The Generative Modeling Problem

The central goal: given samples $$x \sim q(x)$$ from an unknown data distribution $$q(x)$$, learn a model that can generate new samples from $$q(x)$$.

A powerful idea is to learn a **transport map** $$T$$ that transforms a simple, known distribution $$p_0$$ (e.g., Gaussian noise $$\mathcal{N}(0, I)$$) into the data distribution $$q$$:

$$
x_0 \sim p_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, I) \quad \xrightarrow{T} \quad x_1 \sim q(x)
$$

The question is: **how do we construct such a map?**

---

## 2. Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs)

### 2.1 From Discrete to Continuous

Instead of constructing $$T$$ in one step, we define a **continuous path** from noise to data. Consider an ordinary differential equation (ODE) parameterized by a velocity field $$v_t: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$$:

$$
\frac{dx_t}{dt} = v_t(x_t), \quad t \in [0, 1]
$$

Starting from $$x_0 \sim p_0$$, this ODE defines a **flow** $$\phi_t$$ that maps $$x_0$$ to $$x_t = \phi_t(x_0)$$. The flow generates a time-dependent **probability path** $$p_t$$, where:

$$
p_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, I), \quad p_1 \approx q(x)
$$

### 2.2 The Continuity Equation

How does the density $$p_t$$ evolve? By conservation of probability mass, the density satisfies the **continuity equation**:

$$
\frac{\partial p_t(x)}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \left( p_t(x) \, v_t(x) \right) = 0
$$

This tells us that if we choose the right velocity field $$v_t$$, we can steer $$p_0$$ to $$p_1 = q$$.

### 2.3 The Challenge

Training CNFs directly requires solving the ODE during training (to compute log-likelihoods via the instantaneous change of variables formula):

$$
\log p_1(x_1) = \log p_0(x_0) - \int_0^1 \nabla \cdot v_t(\phi_t(x_0)) \, dt
$$

This is computationally expensive. **Flow Matching** and **Diffusion Models** offer more efficient alternatives.

## 3. Diffusion Models from First Principles

### 3.1 The Forward Process: Gradually Destroying Structure

The key insight of diffusion models: instead of directly learning the transport from noise to data, first define a simple **forward process** that gradually adds noise to data.

Define a forward stochastic differential equation (SDE):

$$
dx_t = f(t) \, x_t \, dt + g(t) \, dW_t
$$

where $$W_t$$ is a Wiener process (Brownian motion), $$f(t)$$ is the drift coefficient, and $$g(t)$$ is the diffusion coefficient.

A common choice (Variance Preserving, VP-SDE):

$$
dx_t = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t) \, x_t \, dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)} \, dW_t
$$

The **transition kernel** of this process is Gaussian:

$$
p_{t|0}(x_t | x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \, \alpha_t \, x_0, \, \sigma_t^2 \, I)
$$

where $$\alpha_t = e^{-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^t \beta(s) ds}$$ and $$\sigma_t^2 = 1 - \alpha_t^2$$. As $$t \to \infty$$ (or $$t = T$$ for large $$T$$), $$p_T \approx \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$.

### 3.2 The Reverse Process: Reconstructing Structure

**Anderson's theorem (1982):** the reverse of a diffusion process is also a diffusion:

$$
dx_t = \left[ f(t) \, x_t - g(t)^2 \, \nabla_{x_t} \log p_t(x_t) \right] dt + g(t) \, d\bar{W}_t
$$

where $$\bar{W}_t$$ is a reverse-time Wiener process. The critical unknown: the **score function** $$\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$$.

### 3.3 Score Matching: Learning the Score

We approximate the score with a neural network $$s_\theta(x, t) \approx \nabla_x \log p_t(x)$$. The **explicit score matching** objective:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{ESM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_t \sim p_t} \left[ \left\| s_\theta(x_t, t) - \nabla_{x_t} \log p_t(x_t) \right\|^2 \right]
$$

But $$\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$$ is intractable (it requires marginalizing over all $$x_0$$). 

### 3.4 Denoising Score Matching (DSM)

**Key identity:** we can replace the unknown marginal score with the known **conditional score**:

$$
\nabla_{x_t} \log p_t(x_t) = \mathbb{E}_{x_0 \sim p_{0|t}(x_0|x_t)}\left[\nabla_{x_t} \log p_{t|0}(x_t|x_0)\right]
$$

Since $$p_{t|0}(x_t | x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\alpha_t x_0, \sigma_t^2 I)$$, the conditional score is simply:

$$
\nabla_{x_t} \log p_{t|0}(x_t | x_0) = -\frac{x_t - \alpha_t x_0}{\sigma_t^2} = -\frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}
$$

where $$\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$ and $$x_t = \alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon$$.

The **denoising score matching** objective becomes:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1], \, x_0 \sim q, \, \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)} \left[ \left\| s_\theta(\alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon, t) + \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t} \right\|^2 \right]
$$

Equivalently, reparameterizing $$s_\theta = -\epsilon_\theta / \sigma_t$$, we get the **$$\epsilon$$-prediction** loss (DDPM):

$$
\boxed{\mathcal{L}_{\text{DDPM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, \epsilon} \left[ \left\| \epsilon_\theta(\alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon, t) - \epsilon \right\|^2 \right]}
$$

### 3.5 The Probability Flow ODE

An important result from (Song et al., 2021): for any diffusion SDE, there exists a **deterministic ODE** that induces the same marginal densities $$p_t$$:

$$
\frac{dx_t}{dt} = f(t) x_t - \frac{1}{2} g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x_t)
$$

This connects diffusion models to **Continuous Normalizing Flows** — the probability flow ODE defines a CNF with velocity field:

$$
v_t(x) = f(t) x - \frac{1}{2} g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)
$$

## 4. Flow Matching from First Principles

### 4.1 The Flow Matching Objective

Lipman et al. (2023) proposed a direct approach: instead of going through SDEs, directly regress onto a target velocity field.

Given a target probability path $$p_t$$ with velocity field $$u_t$$, the **Flow Matching (FM)** loss is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1], \, x \sim p_t} \left[ \left\| v_\theta(x, t) - u_t(x) \right\|^2 \right]
$$

**Problem:** computing $$u_t(x)$$ requires knowing the marginal velocity field, which is intractable for the same reason the marginal score is intractable.

### 4.2 Conditional Flow Matching (CFM)

**The key insight:** define the probability path through **conditional paths** $$p_{t|1}(x | x_1)$$, each conditioned on a data sample $$x_1$$:

$$
p_t(x) = \int p_{t|1}(x | x_1) \, q(x_1) \, dx_1
$$

For each conditional path, define a simple interpolation:

$$
\phi_{t|1}(x_0 | x_1) = (1 - t) x_0 + t \, x_1
$$

where $$x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$. This gives conditional distributions:

$$
p_{t|1}(x | x_1) = \mathcal{N}(x; \, t \, x_1, \, (1 - t)^2 I)
$$

The corresponding **conditional velocity field** is:

$$
u_{t|1}(x | x_1) = \frac{x_1 - x}{1 - t}
$$

Or equivalently, along the interpolant $$x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1$$:

$$
u_{t|1}(x_t | x_1) = x_1 - x_0
$$

### 4.3 The CFM Loss

**Theorem (Lipman et al., 2023):** The gradients of the Conditional Flow Matching loss

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{CFM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_1 \sim q, x_0 \sim p_0} \left[ \left\| v_\theta(x_t, t) - u_{t|1}(x_t | x_1) \right\|^2 \right]
$$

are identical to the gradients of the (intractable) FM loss $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}}$$, up to a constant.

Using the linear interpolant, the practical **CFM training objective** is:

$$
\boxed{\mathcal{L}_{\text{CFM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1], \, x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I), \, x_1 \sim q} \left[ \left\| v_\theta\big((1-t)x_0 + t \, x_1, \, t\big) - (x_1 - x_0) \right\|^2 \right]}
$$

This is remarkably simple:
1. Sample noise $$x_0$$ and data $$x_1$$
2. Interpolate: $$x_t = (1 - t)x_0 + t \, x_1$$
3. Predict the velocity $$v_\theta(x_t, t) \approx x_1 - x_0$$
4. Minimize the MSE

### 4.4 Optimal Transport Conditional Flows

The linear interpolation $$x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1$$ with independent coupling $$(x_0, x_1) \sim p_0 \otimes q$$ yields paths that approximate the **optimal transport** displacement interpolation between $$p_0$$ and $$q$$. This results in straighter trajectories compared to diffusion model probability flow ODEs, enabling fewer integration steps at inference.

## 5. Unifying the Two Perspectives

### 5.1 From Diffusion to Flow Matching

The probability flow ODE of a diffusion model defines a velocity field:

$$
v_t^{\text{diff}}(x) = f(t)x - \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)
$$

The corresponding **conditional** velocity field (conditioned on $$x_0$$ in the diffusion convention where $$x_0$$ is data): 

$$
v_t^{\text{diff}}(x_t | x_0) = \frac{d}{dt}\left[\alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon\right] = \dot{\alpha}_t x_0 + \dot{\sigma}_t \epsilon
$$

Both frameworks solve the same generative modeling problem — transporting $$\mathcal{N}(0, I)$$ to $$q(x)$$ — but with different parameterizations:

| | **Diffusion Models** | **Flow Matching** |
|---|---|---|
| **Path** | $$x_t = \alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon$$ | $$x_t = (1-t) x_0 + t \, x_1$$ |
| **Direction** | Data → Noise (forward), Noise → Data (reverse) | Noise → Data |
| **Target** | $$\epsilon$$-prediction or score | Velocity $$x_1 - x_0$$ |
| **Integration** | Often curved trajectories | Straighter trajectories |
| **Steps needed** | Typically 20-1000 | Typically 10-100 |

### 5.2 Gaussian Probability Paths as a Unified Framework 

Both can be viewed as special cases of **Gaussian probability paths**:

$$
p_{t|1}(x | x_1) = \mathcal{N}(x; \, \mu_t(x_1), \, \sigma_t^2 I)
$$

- **VP Diffusion:** $$\mu_t(x_1) = \alpha_t x_1$$, $$\sigma_t = \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t^2}$$
- **Flow Matching (OT):** $$\mu_t(x_1) = t \, x_1$$, $$\sigma_t = 1 - t$$

The conditional velocity field for any such path is:

$$
u_t(x | x_1) = \frac{\dot{\sigma}_t}{\sigma_t}(x - \mu_t(x_1)) + \dot{\mu}_t(x_1)
$$

---

## 6. Summary

Starting from the goal of generative modeling:

1. **CNFs** define a continuous ODE path from noise to data, but are expensive to train.
2. **Diffusion Models** construct a forward noising process, then learn the reverse via score matching. The denoising score matching trick makes training tractable.
3. **Flow Matching** directly regresses a velocity field using conditional flows. The CFM trick makes training tractable with a simple MSE loss.
4. Both are unified under the framework of **Gaussian probability paths** with different schedule choices.

Flow Matching offers conceptual simplicity (no SDEs needed), straighter trajectories (fewer steps), and flexible coupling choices (optimal transport). Diffusion models benefit from a mature ecosystem and well-understood training dynamics.
